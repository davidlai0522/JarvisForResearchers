## TL;DR
We introduce MultiNet v0.1, a systematic evaluation framework benchmarking three state-of-the-art Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models—JAT, GPT-4o, and OpenVLA—across 20 diverse, real-world robotic tasks sourced from the Open-X-Embodiment collection. The evaluation, conducted in a zero-shot setting using Mean Squared Error (MSE) as the primary metric, reveals that while these models show promise, they exhibit significant sensitivity to task complexity and environmental variance, particularly struggling with multi-step planning.

## The Problem
The integration of foundation models into robotics promises a paradigm shift toward general-purpose robotic systems capable of interpreting high-level natural language instructions and executing complex actions. However, the current state of research suffers from a lack of systematic evaluation across diverse robotic tasks. Many existing methodologies constrain foundation models to supervisory roles, effectively treating them as advanced state machines whose outputs are executed by separate, low-level controllers. Furthermore, there is an insufficient, large-scale comparison of different Behavior Cloning (BC) architectures when tested against a heterogeneous set of real-world robotic challenges. This gap prevents a clear understanding of the true generalization capabilities and inherent limitations of contemporary VLA models.

## Key Contributions
This work delivers three primary contributions to the field:
1. Detailed profiling results for an initial set of VLM, VLA, and emerging "generalist" models, providing empirical insights into their operational capabilities and failure modes.
2. A rigorous analysis of model generalization when applied to a diverse suite of real-world robotics datasets, encompassing a wide spectrum of tasks and environmental conditions.
3. The establishment of a systematic set of evaluation splits and metrics specifically tailored for robotics learning tasks within the widely-utilized OpenX Dataset.

The evaluation framework leverages the Open X-Embodiment Dataset (OpenX), which aggregates over one million real robot trajectories captured across 22 distinct robot embodiments. We profile three state-of-the-art models—JAT, GPT-4o, and OpenVLA—across 20 selected datasets. Performance quantification relies primarily on Mean Squared Error (MSE), defined as $\text{MSE} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2$, where $y_i$ is the ground truth action and $\hat{y}_i$ is the predicted action. We also report Average MSE (AMSE) and Normalized AMSE (NAMSE). All models are assessed in a zero-shot configuration, which necessitates specific input preprocessing; for instance, JAT requires 4-channel image inputs, and GPT-4o demands comprehensive prompt engineering.

### JAT
JAT is architecturally defined as a transformer-based model featuring 768-dimensional hidden states and 12 layers. Its operational mechanism incorporates a dual attention strategy: a global attention mechanism operating over a 512-token window, complemented by a local attention mechanism constrained to a 256-token window.

### GPT-4o
GPT-4o functions as an omni-modal model. Its defining characteristic is its capacity to ingest and process heterogeneous inputs—including text, audio, image, and video—while simultaneously generating multi-modal outputs.

### OpenVLA
OpenVLA is an open-source VLA model parameterized by 7 billion parameters. Its structure integrates a 600-million-parameter visual encoder, which is built upon SigLIP and DinoV2 models, with a 7-billion-parameter Llama 2 language model backbone.

## Why This Matters
The findings underscore the critical need for systematic benchmarking when assessing the practical utility of generalist VLA models in robotics. The observed variance in performance across tasks and robot platforms confirms that a monolithic assessment is insufficient; task-specific evaluation is mandatory. Furthermore, the consistent difficulty observed in complex, multi-step planning highlights that current VLA architectures, despite their advanced perceptual and linguistic capabilities, have not yet overcome the fundamental challenge of robust, long-horizon sequential decision-making in unstructured environments.

## Limitations & Open Questions
The current evaluation is inherently constrained by its reliance on offline robotics trajectories; consequently, the models have not been tested on physical hardware. Additionally, certain datasets were necessarily excluded from the analysis due to technical barriers encountered during accessibility via the TensorFlow Datasets (TFDS) builder. Future work must address the transition from simulation/offline data to real-world deployment and explore architectural modifications to improve long-horizon planning capabilities.
