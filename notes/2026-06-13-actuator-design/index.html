    Actuator Design for Robots

    From motor physics to transmission selection, thermal limits, and actuation paradigms — everything needed to specify and design a robot joint actuator, with a worked sizing example for a physical-interaction arm joint.

    1What an actuator must do

    An actuator is the complete chain that converts stored energy into controlled mechanical work at a joint. In a modern robot it is never just "a motor": it is the power electronics, the electromagnetic machine, the transmission, the bearings and structure, and the sensors that close the loop. Designing an actuator means co-designing all five so that the joint delivers the right torque, at the right speed, with the right dynamic behavior (bandwidth, impedance, backdrivability), inside mass, volume, thermal, and cost budgets.

    Central tension Electric motors are naturally fast and weak; robot joints need slow and strong. Almost every design decision is about how to bridge that gap — and what you sacrifice (transparency, efficiency, stiffness, mass, cost) in doing so.

    2Writing the requirements

    Actuator design starts from the load side, never from a motor catalog. For each joint, derive a torque–speed operating envelope from the robot's kinematics, payload, and motion profile:

    • Peak torque τpk — worst-case static gravity torque plus dynamic torque (J·α for accelerations, impacts, disturbance rejection). Typically needed for seconds.
    • Continuous (RMS) torque τrms — computed over the duty cycle; this is what sets the thermal design, and it is the spec beginners miss.
    • Maximum joint speed ωmax and the speed at which peak torque must still be available (corner power point).
    • Bandwidth — how fast the joint must track torque or position commands (manipulation ~5–20 Hz closed-loop; legged locomotion impact response is much higher).
    • Backdrivability / transparency — can the environment move the joint? Critical for contact-rich tasks, safety around humans, and proprioceptive force sensing.
    • Accuracy & stiffness — backlash, torque ripple, positioning repeatability.
    • Envelope budgets — mass (it multiplies down a serial arm: distal actuator mass costs proximal torque), diameter/length, voltage available, thermal environment, noise, ingress protection, cost.

    The RMS torque over a cycle of duration T is:

    τrms = √( (1/T) ∫0T τ(t)² dt )EQ 2.1 — thermal-equivalent torque

    A useful discipline: plot every operating point of the motion profile on the torque–speed plane. The actuator is feasible only if the continuous envelope covers the RMS point and the peak envelope covers every instantaneous point.

    4Electric motor fundamentals

    4.1The two constants that define a motor

    For a permanent-magnet machine (brushed DC, or a BLDC/PMSM under field-oriented control, which behaves like an ideal DC machine in the d–q frame):

    τm = Kt · I      V = I·R + L·dI/dt + Ke · ωmEQ 4.1 — torque & voltage equations

    Kt (N·m/A) maps current to torque; Ke (V·s/rad) is the back-EMF constant. In consistent SI units, Kt = Ke — one physical constant seen from two sides. Consequences:

    • Torque is bought with current → copper (I²R) heating sets the continuous torque limit.
    • Speed is bought with voltage → bus voltage and Ke set the no-load speed ωnl ≈ V/Ke.

    4.2The torque–speed curve

    At fixed voltage, steady-state torque falls linearly with speed:

    τ(ω) = (Kt/R)·(V − Keω) = τstall·(1 − ω/ωnl)EQ 4.2 — torque–speed line

    4.3Copper loss and the motor constant Km

    The dominant loss at robot-joint speeds is resistive heating. Substituting I = τ/Kt:

    Pcu = I²R = τ² · (R/Kt²) = (τ / Km)²,   where  Km ≜ Kt/√REQ 4.3 — copper loss & motor constant

    Km (N·m/√W) is the single best figure of merit for comparing motors: torque per square root of heat. It is independent of winding (rewinding a motor changes Kt and R together, leaving Km fixed) — winding choice only trades voltage against current for the same physics. Compare motors by Km/mass or Km/volume, not by Kt.

    4.4Where torque comes from: geometry

    Torque scales with air-gap area times gap radius — roughly τ ∝ rgap² · L for fixed magnetic and current loading. This is why high-torque robot actuators use large-diameter, short ("pancake") outrunner motors with high pole counts: a big gap radius buys torque directly, reducing the gear ratio needed downstream. Frameless torque-motor kits (rotor + stator supplied without housing) let you integrate the magnetics directly into the joint structure.

    Motor selection heuristics ① Shortlist by Km/kg and diameter fit. ② Check τcont with your heatsinking, not the datasheet's. ③ Check ωnl = Vbus/Ke covers required speed after gearing, with ~20–30% voltage headroom for current-loop authority. ④ Check peak current is within drive and demagnetization limits.

    5Transmissions & reflected inertia

    5.1What a gear ratio buys — and what it costs

    A transmission of ratio N multiplies torque and divides speed:

    τout = N · η · τm     ωout = ωm / NEQ 5.1 — ideal transmission with efficiency η

    But seen from the output, the rotor's inertia and friction are magnified by N²:

    Jreflected = N² · Jrotor     breflected = N² · bmEQ 5.2 — reflected inertia & damping

    Two more classical results follow from the N² law:

    • Inertia matching: for maximum load acceleration with a given motor, choose N = √(Jload/Jrotor) — the ratio that makes reflected rotor inertia equal load inertia. Real designs often sit below this (favoring transparency) or above it (favoring static torque).
    • Torque sensing fidelity: with low N, motor current is an honest estimate of joint torque (proprioception); with high N, friction and inertia swamp it, forcing a separate torque sensor or spring.

    6Actuation paradigms

    Combining the motor and transmission choices yields four recognizable design philosophies. The axis that separates them is how the actuator handles contact with the world.

    6.1Direct drive (N = 1)

    Motor torque = joint torque. Perfect transparency, zero backlash, current ≈ torque exactly. But torque per mass is poor — to hold meaningful loads the motors become large, heavy, and hot (continuous torque costs (τ/Km)² in heat). Used where transparency matters more than gravity loads: haptic devices, some SCARA arms, gimbals.

    6.2Quasi-direct drive / proprioceptive actuation (N ≈ 5–10)

    The MIT Mini Cheetah pattern, now ubiquitous in legged robots and low-cost arms: a large-gap-radius outrunner (exploiting τ ∝ rgap²) plus a single planetary stage. Reflected inertia stays small (N² = 25–100, not 10,000), so the joint is backdrivable, survives impacts by letting the rotor accelerate, and senses contact through motor current alone — "proprioceptive" force control with no extra sensor. Costs: lower torque density than harmonic designs, more heat for the same continuous torque, large diameter joints.

    6.3High-ratio geared (harmonic / cycloidal, N = 50–160)

    The industrial-arm and cobot standard: a compact frameless motor plus a strain-wave or cycloidal stage gives outstanding torque density and stiffness with near-zero backlash. Transparency is gone — so cobots (KUKA iiwa, Franka) add a joint torque sensor after the gearbox and close a torque loop to fake backdrivability in software. Limits: torque control bandwidth is bounded by structural/gear dynamics, and unsensed collisions are felt through N² inertia before control reacts.

    6.4Series elastic actuation (SEA)

    Insert a known spring (stiffness ks) between gearbox and load and measure its deflection: τ = ks·Δθ. The spring turns torque control into position control of deflection, filters impacts, and stores energy (great for walking). The cost is bandwidth — the motor must move the spring before torque changes, so force bandwidth falls with softer springs. Classic in humanoids (early Valkyrie, Baxter) and prosthetics/exoskeletons.

    7Sensing & drive electronics

    7.1Field-oriented control (FOC)

    A BLDC/PMSM is driven by a three-phase inverter. FOC transforms phase currents into the rotor frame (Clarke/Park transforms) and regulates the torque-producing current iq with a PI loop running at 10–40 kHz. Result: smooth torque proportional to iq, just like a DC machine. The cascade above it is conventional: current (kHz) → velocity (hundreds of Hz) → position (tens–hundreds of Hz), each loop ~5–10× slower than the one inside it. Modern integrated driver boards (e.g. moteus, ODrive-class, or vendor servo drives) make this a solved subsystem — your job is mostly parameter identification and tuning.

    7.2Encoders

    • Rotor-side encoder (mandatory): commutation + velocity. Magnetic off-axis or on-axis (e.g. 14–17 bit) is standard; optical for precision.
    • Output-side absolute encoder (recommended for arms): measures the joint after backlash/compliance, gives true link angle and absolute startup position. Dual-encoder joints can also estimate transmission deflection → crude torque sensing.
    • Joint torque sensor (cobots): strain-gauge structure after the gearbox; adds cost and structural compliance but enables high-fidelity torque control through opaque transmissions.

    7.3Electrical system notes

    • Bus voltage sets the speed ceiling (ωnl = V/Ke); 24/48 V is typical for arms and small robots. Higher voltage → lower current → thinner cables, less I²R in wiring.
    • Regeneration: a decelerating joint pumps energy back into the bus — budget a brake resistor or battery that can absorb it, or the bus voltage will spike.
    • Current limits live in three places: motor demagnetization, drive FETs, and supply. The firmware limit must respect all three.

    8Thermal design

    Continuous torque is a thermal spec. The winding temperature rise above ambient is, in the simplest lumped model:

    ΔT = Ploss · Rth = (τrms/Km)² · (Rth,wh + Rth,ha)EQ 8.1 — lumped thermal model

    where Rth,wh is winding→housing and Rth,ha housing→ambient thermal resistance (K/W). Design levers, in order of leverage:

    1. Lower the heat: pick a higher-Km motor or raise N (heat falls with the square of required motor torque).
    2. Shrink Rth,ha: bolt the stator to structure with real conducted area; the robot's frame is your heatsink. Potting windings helps Rth,wh.
    3. Exploit transients: thermal time constants are tens of seconds (winding) to minutes (housing) — peak torque bursts are fine if the RMS is safe. Implement an I²t thermal observer in firmware rather than a hard current cap.
    4. Remember R rises with temperature (copper +0.4%/K): a hot motor produces less torque per volt and more heat per amp — thermal runaway is real near the limit.

    9The design procedure

    Feasibility conditions to satisfy simultaneously (with efficiency η and margin factors):

    N·η·τm,pk ≥ τpk   ·   N·η·τm,cont ≥ τrms   ·   ωnl/N ≥ ~1.3·ωmaxEQ 9.1 — feasibility set

    10Worked example: a physical-interaction arm joint

    Size the elbow/shoulder-class joint of an assistive arm that must support and reposition a limb-scale load while remaining backdrivable enough for safe human contact.

    SIZING WALKTHROUGH — assistive arm joint
    01
    Requirements. Gravity load: 10 kg effective mass at 0.5 m lever → τg ≈ 10·9.81·0.5 ≈ 49 N·m. Add dynamics and margin: τpk = 60 N·m. Duty-cycle RMS over a transfer-assist motion (hold + move + idle): τrms = 25 N·m. Max joint speed ωmax = 2 rad/s (≈115°/s — deliberately slow around people). Backdrivability required → cap the ratio at N ≲ 30; safety argues against harmonic-only designs without torque sensing.
    02
    Candidate motor. Frameless outrunner, Kt = 0.11 N·m/A, R = 0.15 Ω (→ Km = 0.28 N·m/√W), τcont = 1.2 N·m (heatsunk), τpk = 3.5 N·m, Jrotor = 1.2×10⁻⁴ kg·m².
    03
    Gear ratio. With two-stage planetary η ≈ 0.85: peak needs N ≥ 60/(0.85·3.5) ≈ 20.2; continuous needs N ≥ 25/(0.85·1.2) ≈ 24.5. Choose N = 25.
    04
    Speed check. Motor speed at ωmax: 25 × 2 = 50 rad/s ≈ 478 rpm — trivially low; even a 24 V bus gives ωnl = 24/0.11 ≈ 218 rad/s, a 4× margin. ✔ (We could trade: a lower-Kt winding would lower current; here current is the binding constraint instead.)
    05
    Currents. Peak motor torque 60/(25·0.85) = 2.82 N·m → Ipk = 2.82/0.11 ≈ 26 A (drive must supply this). Continuous 25/(25·0.85) = 1.18 N·m → Icont ≈ 10.7 A.
    06
    Thermal check. Pcu = (1.18/0.28)² ≈ 17.8 W. With stator bolted to the joint casting, Rth ≈ 2 K/W → ΔT ≈ 36 K: winding ~60–65 °C in a 25 °C room. ✔ but note τcont usage is at 98% of rating — no margin. Action: either accept with an I²t observer and conservative duty assumptions, step up one motor size, or add a parallel gravity-compensation spring to cut τrms (often the single best trick for joints that hold loads against gravity).
    07
    Reflected inertia & transparency. Jrefl = 25² × 1.2×10⁻⁴ = 0.075 kg·m² — comparable to the link's own inertia: moderately backdrivable, adequate current-based collision detection, much safer than an N = 100 harmonic joint (which would reflect 1.2 kg·m²). For higher contact fidelity, add an output torque sensor or drop to N ≈ 10 with a larger motor.
    08
    Spec the rest. Crossed-roller output bearing (moment loads), 14-bit rotor encoder + absolute output encoder, 48 V bus to halve currents if cabling is long, FOC drive with ≥30 A peak, brake resistor sized for the worst lowering-a-load regen event, and — for an assistive device — a power-off brake or self-locking backup path so the joint fails safe under gravity.

    11Safety considerations for physical HRI

    When the actuator works on or around people, design choices become safety arguments:

    • Limit energy, not just force. ISO/TS 15066-style thinking bounds transferred energy and pressure in contact; low reflected inertia (low N) directly reduces impact severity before any controller reacts.
    • Layered torque limits: firmware current clamp → drive hardware limit → mechanical fuse/slip clutch. Software limits alone are not a safety case.
    • Fail-safe under gravity: power-off brakes, self-locking stages, or counterbalancing so loss of power never drops the load.
    • Collision detection: momentum-observer or current-residual methods work well on transparent (QDD) joints; geared joints need torque sensors for comparable sensitivity.
    • Redundant sensing (dual encoders, cross-checked) for any joint whose failure can injure.
